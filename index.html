Resonant Politics: Lebanese Sound and Music Across Three Eras

Farah Noureddine

How often do politics play a role in shaping sound, culture, and collective memory? This question drives my inquiry into Lebanese music as a case study for understanding the intricate relationships between political violence, cultural production, and collective consciousness. This project examines why Lebanese music keeps returning to the same themes, feelings, and expressions of anger and grief across decades–not through repetition, but through a form of resonance that carries political knowledge across generations.

Lebanon's modern history has been marked by cycles of violence, reconstruction, and collapse. The fifteen-year civil war (1975-1990) resulted in massive casualties and displacement (MERIP, 1990). The postwar period promised reconstruction but delivered mounting debt, infrastructure decay, and preserved sectarian divisions. Contemporary Lebanon has faced economic collapse, political paralysis, the catastrophic 2020 port explosion (Kim et al., 2022), and most recently, the 2024 war on Lebanon that killed over 3,900 people and displaced 1.2 million Lebanese civilians (Council on Foreign Relations, 2025).

Lebanese music simultaneously documents political conditions and constitutes political action itself. When Ziad Rahbani smuggled cassette tapes of his satirical plays across civil war checkpoints, he performed politics through sound (Asmar, 2013). When Mashrou' Leila sang openly about queer desire in a region where LGBTQ+ rights face severe restrictions, they created space for existence and resistance (CNN, 2017). When protesters in 2019 resurrected songs from decades earlier, they recognized that the political conditions those songs critiqued remain unchanged (Ipek, 2020).

I examine three distinct Lebanese political eras: the civil war era (1975-1990), the false prosperity era (1990-2010), and the modern era (2010-present). Each period produced its own sonic landscape, yet these soundscapes remain in constant dialogue, creating a continuous thread of political consciousness transmitted through music. This temporal layering makes Lebanese music a rich site for understanding how sound carries political knowledge across time, what Hirsch (2012) terms "postmemory"–forms of inherited trauma that shape identity and consciousness even for those who didn't directly experience originating events.

About the Author

Farah is a Master's student in the Communication and Culture Joint Program at Toronto Metropolitan University. Her research interests lie at the intersection of pop culture, politics, and media. This research paper grew out of a genuine curiosity: does a political environment shape the music that gets made? Farah believes it does, but she'll let you read and decide for yourself.
