Era Two: False Prosperity and Collective Amnesia (1990-2010)

When the war officially ended in 1990 with the Taif Agreement, Lebanon made a collective decision: don't talk about what happened. The agreement, while ending armed conflict, preserved Lebanon's sectarian power-sharing system. The Taif Agreement "maintained the old arrangement with some adjustments, allowing for an equitable distribution of top-level civil service posts and of parliamentary seats between Christians and Muslims" (Carnegie Endowment for International Peace, 2016). As Haugbolle (2005) observes, Lebanon embraced "political amnesia" dressed up as reconstruction–a conscious forgetting mandated by the very political structure that had enabled the war. Although the Taif Agreement identified the abolition of political sectarianism as a national priority, it provided no timeframe for doing so, and these reforms were never implemented (Conciliation Resources, 2012). The postwar generation "developed in an environment where discussing the war was taboo" (Burkhalter, 2012).

This era witnessed what might be called the "professionalization" of Lebanese music, with increased investment in studio production, music videos, and regional distribution. The rise of satellite television created new circulation networks for Arabic pop music, and Lebanese artists positioned themselves to capitalize on these opportunities. As Cestor (2010) notes, this period saw the flourishing of talent shows like Studio El Fan on LBC, which "launched the careers of major pop singers" and created what I argue was a parallel reality where life went on "normally" (Cestor, 2010). Music became part of a broader reconstruction project that emphasized consumerism. Yet this glossy surface masked economic realities that would eventually explode. The postwar government, led by Prime Minister Rafik Hariri, pursued a reconstruction model based on massive debt and real estate speculation. Music's refusal to address these mounting structural problems wasn't apolitical–it was profoundly political, serving to naturalize an unsustainable and deeply unjust status quo.

This sonic landscape of forgetting represents what Goh might critique as a departure from "situated knowledges" toward a false universalism that erases "partiality" and "positioning" (Goh, 2017). The glossy pop music of this era attempted to transcend Lebanon's traumatic history, offering a depoliticized sound that could circulate regionally without acknowledging the specific conditions of its production. Yet this very erasure was itself political, serving the postwar state's project of enforced amnesia.

The year 2005 disrupted this carefully maintained forgetting. When former Prime Minister Rafik Hariri was assassinated on February 14, massive protests brought people into the streets. Suddenly, the sonic landscape transformed. Competing political party anthems, speeches amplified through massive sound systems, and nationalist songs filled public space. The assassination generated immediate musical responses. Songs like "Aal Waad Nkammel Darbak" (2005), which literally translates to "We promise to continue your path" emerged, pledging to continue Hariri's legacy. Political factions aligned themselves with musical expressions of loyalty and resistance. Julia Boutros released "Ahibaai" (2006) a song post the 2006 war with Israel, giving support to Hezbollah and their leader Hassan Nasrallah.

Political factions deployed music strategically, each trying to claim sonic dominance (Khatib, 2022). Yet the ultimate outcome–Syrian withdrawal without fundamental restructuring of Lebanon's sectarian political system–meant that the underlying conditions producing conflict remained intact. The "affective" power of music that Robinson and Nickleson describe (2023) was mobilized not for liberation but for sectarian mobilization, demonstrating how the same sonic strategies can serve radically different political ends depending on their "positioning" within power structures (Goh, 2017).

Ghassan Rahbani's "Saret Senet el 2000" (2000), which translates to "It's now the year 2000," was a satirical take on the climate of the country then. Rahbani sings, "It's now the year 2000, and the country is finally great … there's no problems anymore, and our minds are completely clear." Which of course, was not the case. The song would be remade twenty years later by Blu Fiefer in 2020, playing on the same words but with a starkly different meaning; two decades later, the same phrases resonated with irony and disillusionment, showing how little had changed despite the passage of time. Fiefer here sings, "It's now 2020, and the country is fucked, and the Lira (Lebanese currency) has collapsed." This sonic recycling across two decades illustrates how Lebanese music functions as a form of collective memory, with each generation reinterpreting the same materials through their own "situated" experience (Goh, 2017).

The postwar era thus oscillated between enforced amnesia and explosive return of political music, between glossy depoliticization and naked sectarian mobilization. What remained absent was sustained engagement with the war's unresolved traumas and the structural conditions that had enabled violence in the first place. The underground improvisation scene kept this engagement alive, but in sonic forms accessible mainly to a small, educated elite. The broader public's "feeling of knowing" the war (Robinson and Nickleson, 2023) remained largely unprocessed, stored in sonic archives waiting for the right moment to resurface.
