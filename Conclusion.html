Conclusion: Unresolved Resonances

Lebanese music doesn't just reflect political reality–it shapes how we know it, how we feel it, how we remember it, and how we refuse to forget it. The songs carry forward political consciousness across decades, creating what I call resonant politics–forms of knowledge and feelings that echo across time, generation after generation finding themselves singing the same songs, grieving the same losses, demanding the same changes.

As long as the political conditions remain unresolved, the songs will keep resonating. The civil war never truly ended; it transformed into different manifestations of the same structural violence. The postwar amnesia merely deferred confrontation with the past. The October 17 Revolution made visible what music had been articulating all along–that Lebanon's political system is fundamentally unsustainable, built on corruption, sectarianism, and exploitation of ordinary people by entrenched elites. The 2024 war proved the cycle continues.

Music became one way Lebanese people maintained sanity, built community, articulated dissent, and preserved alternative political visions across eras when explicit political organizing faced severe constraints. From Ziad Rahbani smuggling cassettes across the Green Line, to Mashrou' Leila asserting queer existence, to El Rass deconstructing political language through rap – Lebanese music has consistently performed political work that bodies alone couldn't safely do.

The theoretical frameworks that guided this analysis–Goh's "situated knowledges" and Robinson and Nickleson's "feeling of knowing music"–help us understand why this matters (Goh; Robinson and Nickleson). Music isn't just documentation of politics; it's a form of political knowledge production itself. It teaches us how to listen to power, how to recognize its disguises, how to imagine otherwise. As Goh argues, this knowledge emerges from specific "positioning" within political structures, requiring us to acknowledge "partiality" and reject false claims to universality (Goh, 2017). As Robinson and Nickleson (2023) insist, this knowledge cannot be separated from feeling–"knowing and feeling are inseparable" in music. Lebanese music transmits both political analysis and embodied experience of injustice, both critique of power and visceral sense of what it feels like to live under its weight.

Lebanese music keeps returning to the same themes not because it's trapped in the past but because the past remains brutally present. The songs resonate across decades because they carry truths that political discourse often obscures: that sectarianism serves elite interests, that corruption flows from the very structure of the state, that ordinary people have been sacrificed repeatedly to maintain a fundamentally unjust system. Until these conditions change, the music will keep saying so–in melody and rhythm, in protest and mourning, in anger and love and refusal to accept that this is all Lebanon can be. “We won't forget, we won't reconcile, we won't compromise, we won't shake hands”–the refrain from the Keserwany siblings echoes across Beirut still, a sonic refusal that resonates with every generation that has watched their city burn, waited for change, and refused to stop singing.
