Era One: The Civil War (1975-1990)

The Lebanese Civil War didn't just destroy infrastructure–it shattered collective existence and reorganized social space. Lebanon's people paid "a tremendous price for 15 years of invasion and civil war," with casualties resulting from "almost daily shelling, sniper fire, murder and other indiscriminate acts" (MERIP, 1990). Beirut became divided by the Green Line, with checkpoints separating predominantly Christian East Beirut from predominantly Muslim West Beirut. Yet music became one of the few things that could cross those barriers, even if only through smuggling and radio waves. As Burkhalter (2012) notes, the civil war period witnessed "rock and protest music, and the noises and sounds of bombs, radio, and propaganda" competing for sonic space in a fragmented city.

The war shaped Lebanese collective memory in ways extending far beyond those who lived through it. Listening to music from that era produces what Hirsch (2012) calls "postmemory"–knowledge transmitted across generations about traumatic events one never directly experienced. This concept helps explain how music functions as what Goh would term a form of "situated knowledge" (Goh, 2017), carrying embodied understanding of trauma across time. The songs don't simply represent the war; they transmit the "feeling of knowing" what it meant to live through it (Robinson and Nickleson, 2023).

Ziad Rahbani emerged as one of Lebanon's most important cultural figures during the war years–a composer, singer, and political satirist who understood sound's capacity to do work that bodies couldn't safely do. During the civil war, Ziad recorded political plays and music in West Beirut, but he wanted audiences in East Beirut to hear them. So he would record his works onto cassette tapes and distribute them through unconventional channels. Sami Asmar (2013) describes how Ziad's work challenged "the centrality of sectarianism to the Lebanese national narrative," using humor and music to locate injustice "within a wider network of inequalities" rather than reducing everything to communal conflict. His smuggled tapes became tools for maintaining an alternative political consciousness, functioning as what Goh (2017) might describe as "discourse" that operates through "embodied" listening practices rather than purely rational argument. The act of listening to Ziad's smuggled cassettes was itself a political act, one that required acknowledging one's "positioning" within the sectarian geography of divided Beirut.

Beyond Ziad's satirical interventions, the civil war witnessed the emergence of what Hodeib (2017) calls "leftist militant songs"–a genre that developed at the intersection of radical theatre, poetry, and music during the 1970s. These productions were "spontaneous organic representations of the revolutionary discourse" while simultaneously serving as "instrumental tools to reproduce radical ideas in mass culture" (Hodeib, 2017). The songs functioned both as expressions of political commitment and as pedagogical interventions, teaching listeners new ways of understanding their social position and political possibilities.

Many revolutionary songs of the period emerged alongside Israel's occupation of southern Lebanon, especially during the late years of the civil war and the continued occupation into the 1990s till the year 2000. Ziad's "The National Lebanese Resistance Song" (1985) became a song of loss, occupation and resistance for decades to come. Marcel Khalife became one of the most iconic voices of this era with songs like "Walking Tall" (1985) (based on poetry by Palestinian poet Samih Al-Qasim). "Walking Tall" in particular became an anthem of resistance, with its lyrics about walking upright with dignity despite oppression. In this song Khalife says, "I carry an olive branch in my palm, and my coffin on my shoulders", which mainly summarizes this period in one sentence. The conjunction of Lebanese and Palestinian revolutionary movements created fertile ground for this musical innovation. Artists used songs to articulate visions of anti-colonial struggle and revolutionary transformation. Their work embodied what Williams (1977) called "structures of feeling"–emergent political sensibilities not yet fully articulated in formal political discourse but palpable in cultural production. Another song from this era is Ahmad Kaabour's "Ounadikom" (1976) (written by Tawfiq Zayyad), which is a ballad to resistance fighters, in which he says, “I kiss the ground, underneath your boots, and sacrifice myself for you”.

Fairuz's voice became synonymous with a Beirut that no longer existed during the war. Her song "Li Beirut" (1984) expressed profound longing for the city being destroyed, singing of Beirut as a beloved that has been taken away. She sings, “My city has extinguished its lantern, She [Beirut] closed her door, Became at night alone, Alone with the night” (Raseef22, 2019). This nostalgic mourning for a lost city would echo through Lebanese music for decades, establishing a template for how artists would express loss and longing for peace. Fairuz's music exemplifies what Robinson and Nickleson describe as the inseparability of "knowing and feeling"–her songs don't simply inform listeners about Beirut's destruction, they make them feel the loss in ways that "shared affective and loving experiences of music" can uniquely accomplish (Robinson and Nickleson, 2023).

Decades later, Yasmine Hamdan released "Beirut" (2014). Hamdan offers a striking counterpoint to Fairuz's classic "Li Beirut." Where Fairuz sang of nostalgia and longing for a lost city, Hamdan's Beirut is grittier, more ambiguous, acknowledging both love and pain, beauty and destruction. Hamdan sings, “Beirut Oh her beauty, her good old days, that dire end, all a waste, Withering”. Both songs, however, share what Robinson and Nickleson describe as the capacity to make listeners feel what it means to know Beirut–not as abstract knowledge but as "shared affective and loving experiences" transmitted through sound (Robinson and Nickleson, 2023).

The civil war era thus established sonic patterns and political vocabularies that would persist across decades. Music became a repository of "situated knowledge" (Goh, 2017) about living under violence, carrying forward both explicit political messages and the embodied experience of fear, hope, resistance, and longing. These songs created what we might call a sonic archive of political consciousness, ready to be reactivated whenever similar conditions returned.
