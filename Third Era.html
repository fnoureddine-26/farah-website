Era Three: The Modern Era (2010-Present)

Everything the false prosperity era tried to bury erupted during this period. The 2011 Arab Spring sent shockwaves through the region, sparking renewed political consciousness particularly among youth. This era saw the emergence of entirely new music scenes in Lebanon: rap, R&B, alternative rock, underground electronic music. These represented new ways of producing political knowledge through music, new sonic strategies for articulating dissent (Burkhalter, 2012).

Mashrou' Leila became perhaps the most internationally prominent example. Formed in 2008 at the American University of Beirut during a music workshop (Neumeier, 2025), the band brought queer politics and social critique into indie rock in unprecedented ways. Their lyrics addressed sectarianism, gender equality, and homophobia. Sinno explained: "music is always inherently political. People just have a tendency to make the facile distinction that music is only political when the politics of it are politics of dissent" (CNN, 2017). This insight captures something crucial about the "situated" nature of all musical knowledge (Goh, 2017)–the political work of music isn't limited to explicit protest songs. Mashrou' Leila's articulation of queer existence constituted political intervention in a society structured by compulsory heterosexuality, sectarianism, and patriarchy. They were banned in Jordan multiple times (Neumeier, 2025). After rainbow flags appeared at their Cairo concert in 2017, Egyptian authorities arrested dozens of concert-goers and subjected some to forced anal examinations (Amnesty International, 2017). In 2019, Lebanon's Byblos festival cancelled their concert after accusations of blasphemy from church leaders (Neumeier, 2025). The band eventually disbanded in 2022 following sustained online harassment and the COVID-19 pandemic.

In 2015, Lebanon's garbage crisis brought thousands into the streets. Called for by grassroots activists using the hashtag #YouStink, the protests formed "an embryonic civic movement against the sectarian political system" (Carnegie Endowment for International Peace, 2015). The protests represented rejection of "the country's sectarianism, its political elite, and the corrupt practices it represents." At the heart of this crisis lay "Lebanon's political system, which associates political representation with sectarian affiliation" (Carnegie Endowment for International Peace, 2015). The garbage piling up across Beirut became literal evidence of state failure and the corruption inherent in the sectarian quota system.

Everything culminated on October 17, 2019, when Lebanon exploded into its largest mass protests. Triggered initially by a proposed tax on WhatsApp calls, the demonstrations quickly evolved into comprehensive rejection of the political establishment under the slogan "Kullun Ya'ni Kullun" ("All of them means all of them"). Nearly two million people participated–approximately one-third of the country's population (Ipek, 2020). The October 17 Revolution produced its own distinct sonic culture. Musicians and DJs became central to protest culture. Rappers contributed music that articulated protesters' rage.

El Rass (Mazen El Sayed) emerged as Lebanese hip-hop's most intellectually rigorous voice. A journalist turned rapper from Tripoli, El Rass crafts lyrics in both classical and colloquial Arabic. His approach is "an anthropologist or political analyst, using rap as his chosen medium of expression" (Scene Noise, 2024). El Rass articulates rap's political function: "What we do is really about trying to be faster and more dynamic than the people we are fighting. It is about inventing new words. It is about inventing new formulas. It is about trying to deconstruct meanings of words in the minds of people and replace them" (Heinrich Böll Foundation, 2015). His songs "Shouf" and "Ali Sha'ib" exemplify this approach. "Shouf" (2019) was released during the October 17 Revolution, in it he sings, "Walid (Jumblatt) and Samir (Geagea) and Sami (Gemayel) think I'm stupid, but they've forgotten that now we're all revolting", directly naming political figures and calling out their assumptions about the passivity of ordinary people. This direct naming of power, combined with the assertion of collective awakening, represents a bold challenge to political elites. In "Ali Sha'ib" (2020), El Rass sings about the economic collapse post the October 17 Revolution, "Beirut your walls are just like biscuits, being broken into pieces and given away". In here he addresses how public spaces have all turned private, and how political parties are the biggest beneficiaries of the economic collapse, by giving away pieces of Beirut.

Other artists contributed to this expanding sonic landscape. Bu Nasser Touffar raps about revenge in "Tar" (2020), he says, "All I sleep, wake up, eat, drink and work is dipped in blood". His song was released post the October 17 Revolution and the Beirut explosion, to describe how the general population was in a state of wanting revenge from the political class. What unites these diverse musical forms is their commitment to what Goh calls "situated knowledges" (2017)–they speak from specific positions within Lebanese society, acknowledging their "partiality" rather than claiming false universality (Goh, 2017). They embody what Robinson and Nickleson describe as music that refuses to separate "knowing and feeling" (2023), using sound to transmit both political analysis and embodied experience of injustice.

The Beirut Port explosion on August 4, 2020, represents a rupture that still reverberates. At approximately 6:07 PM, 2,750 tons of ammonium nitrate exploded with force comparable to 1.1 kilotons of TNT–one of the largest non-nuclear explosions in recorded history (Kim et al., 2022). The explosion's sound itself became evidence–captured in countless videos, analyzed by seismologists, embedded in collective trauma. Survivors describe the sound as unlike anything else. One witness wrote: "There was something about the sound of the blast that day and how it knocked my mother and I off our chairs... The way it cracked through the air, it felt final. It felt like something you don't survive" (The National, 2025).

In the explosion's aftermath, many artists fell silent. Others tried to capture the incomprehensible. Postcards released "Home Is So Sad" (2021) a haunting meditation on loss and displacement that many Beirutis felt after the blast destroyed their homes and neighborhoods. They take us on an agonizing description of the aftermath of the explosion: "Glass in our coffee, Towels on trees, Blood from your nostrils, Blood from your ears". Michelle and Noel Keserwany released "Alf Leileh w Leil" (2021), which translates to One Thousand and One Night. This duo has always been known for their smart and witty take on politics through songs. In the end of this song, they keep on repeating one phrase, "We won't forget, we won't reconcile, we won't compromise, we won't shake hands", referring to the explosion and what's to come after.

The blast materialized all the corruption and indifference that had characterized Lebanon's political class, making audible what had long been known but somehow not fully real–that the state could and would let its citizens die through negligence. The explosion's sound became what Goh might term the ultimate "situated knowledge" (2017)–knowledge that could only be known through the embodied experience of that specific moment, in that specific place, yet which also transmitted political truths about state negligence and the disposability of ordinary Lebanese lives.

The 2024 war on Lebanon represents the most recent chapter in Lebanon's ongoing crisis. Beginning with cross-border exchanges in October 2023 and escalating dramatically in September 2024, the conflict killed over 3,900 people in Lebanon, injured over 16,000, and displaced approximately 1.2 million Lebanese civilians (Council on Foreign Relations, 2025). Israeli strikes destroyed or damaged extensive infrastructure, with entire villages in southern Lebanon experiencing massive destruction. The sonic landscape of the 2024 conflict introduced elements that distinguish it from previous wars: the constant presence of drone sounds, the specific acoustic signatures of precision-guided munitions, and the psychological impact of explosions. These sounds became part of daily life for millions of Lebanese, particularly in southern Lebanon, Beirut's southern suburbs, and the Beqaa Valley (House of Commons Library, 2024).

The return of war sounds to Lebanese life–sounds that many young Lebanese had only experienced through their parents' stories or through music about the civil war–created temporal collapse. The drones overhead, the explosions, the sirens all connected directly to the sonic memories embedded in civil war-era music. This wasn't metaphorical resonance but literal sonic continuity: the same sounds of bombardment, displacement, and state failure that had shaped Lebanese music for decades returned with renewed intensity. Perhaps most strikingly, songs from the civil war era resurfaced with renewed relevance. Ziad Rahbani's "The National Lebanese Resistance Song" composed decades earlier, began circulating again. The song's reemergence demonstrated how Lebanese music functions as a repository of political consciousness, ready to be activated whenever similar conditions return. In this song, there's a line that says, "the number of martyrs has neither decreased nor increased", referring to those residing in the South of Lebanon, and how they will always remain an afterthought in Lebanese politics–just numbers. This recycling exemplifies what Goh describes as "situated knowledge" that remains relevant precisely because the "positioning" of Lebanese people within structures of violence has not fundamentally changed (Goh, 2017).
